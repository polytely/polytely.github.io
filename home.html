How can energy efficiency improvements be fostered?

Let’s start with the basics: If we wanted to build a solution that supports energy consumers to become more energy efficient, what should our primary goal be?

  Do consumers need more information about their energy consumption?

We already know that detailed feedback on electricity consumption that provides real-time or near real-time information, enables consumers to save energy through reduced waste. But how the feedback is provided, the way consumers perceive this information, whether they believe that they are capable of making a difference and whether they are motivated to take action are the really critical issues.

  Should consumers devote time and energy to be more responsive to the grid’s needs?

A person that uses his/her car to drive from place A to place B does not and should not care about the fact that the car is full of microprocessors that make operational decisions – at a speed that is much faster than the driver’s reaction time – regarding aspects that support but do not interfere with the transport service provided.

This is a good analogy for demand response; consumers need specific services (e.g. lighting or heating) and all aspects of optimizing shiftable loads should take place under the hood. In other words, demand response should be as automated as possible. But we should not confuse energy efficiency with demand response.

  What is really needed for energy efficiency?

The aim of energy efficiency is to support the same level of energy service either with less energy consumption or from renewable energy sources. In contrast with demand response, energy efficiency improvements require conscious decisions and agency. Agency is created when people believe that their contribution is important, know what to do and decide for themselves to do it. Furthermore, efficiency-related decisions are mainly investment decisions.

Motivating and supporting consumers’ behavioral change to achieve greater energy efficiency requires providing an intrinsic value to energy efficiency, as well as providing awareness of the existence of alternative options to improve it when the corresponding windows of investment opportunity materialize.

Coming back to the original question, our primary goal should be to empower consumers to pursuit energy efficiency improvements. Consumers feel empowered if they can pursuit a goal that is aligned with their perceptions of personal and community level welfare, quality of life and responsibility, as well as if they are supported to achieve gradual and persistent changes in their energy consumption profile.

The role of data analytics in creating energy efficiency services

Big Data is a term consistently found in articles and solutions concerning energy efficiency, the premise being that smart meters – and smart grids in general – are able to collect and make available large volumes of data, the analysis of which can help identify relationships that were previously unnoticed, improve predictability and facilitate new services of information provision. Although this premise is generally true, it helps if we have already defined the questions that our data analytics algorithms should be able to address. In Polytely, we are focusing on two challenges that are critical, according to our way of perceiving the energy efficiency services to provide.

The first challenge is to extract of set of recurring patterns in a stream of energy consumption data from a large number of households, so as to perform meaningful clustering of these households. Contrary to the aggregated load curves which are characterized by their seasonality, regularity and sensitivity to meteorological conditions, household load curves are very volatile, their shape depends mainly on the consumer behaviour and are less dependent on weather conditions. Consequently, our focus is on identifying and exploiting common consumption behaviour patterns in a load profiles stream.

In reality, this is a feature extraction problem and in this context meaningful clustering implies the ability to associate recurring patterns in consumption with actual practices in the consumers's daily routines. Intelligent efficiency is largely based on the premise that clustering by recurrent patterns will lead to results that are significantly similar to the actual segmentation of the consumers based on their habits and characteristics.

The second challenge concerns the estimation of the willingness or capability of the consumers to adapt their consumption profiles in response to external recommendations. One way to approach this issue is as if each consumer can be represented by one model that generates a load profile segment based on the load profile segment’s class during the previous time interval and by another model that generates deviations in this load profile as a response to a recommendation received.

Both challenges must be addressed if one wants to 'translate' the information gathered into actual guidance provision towards energy efficiency goals.

Real time coordination of energy demand, storage and supply

If we focus only on load shifting, demand response is constrained by the fact that there is a limited potential for shifting load to a ‘better’ time. However, the main goal of demand response is making the best use of available resources, rather than load shifting or curtailment. In fact, demand response can be regarded as a solar and wind balancing mechanism.

Under this perspective, demand response is related to coordinating the capabilities of neighboring building blocks. The heterogeneity in capabilities makes this coordination challenging, but it is necessary for achieving a high level of sufficiency or flexibility. The ability of micro-generation and storage enabled buildings to contribute – through aggregation – to demand response is a key enabler so that multiple homes can provide energy services when they are needed. Heat pumps can provide electricity demand reduction, even more when combined with thermal storage. Micro combined heat and power (Micro-CHP) can provide additional generation capacity at peak times and solar PV with storage can store electricity when generation is high but demand low, for use when system demand peaks either to export or for self consumption.

Model predictive control (MPC) refers to a class of control algorithms that utilise an internal model to predict the future response of a process. At each control interval, an MPC algorithm optimises the future process behaviour by computing a sequence of future inputs.

We are exploring ways to combine MPC schemes with machine learning, since machine learning algorithms can identify the important features in the data and then feed them into an MPC scheme.
