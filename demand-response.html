Using data analytics to support Automated Demand Response

Demand Response programs aim for changes in daily electric usage – relatively to the consumers’ normal consumption patterns – in response to changes in the price of electricity over time or to incentive payments designed to induce lower electricity use at times of high wholesale market prices or when system reliability is jeopardized.

Demand response can be provided by consumers through:

  • Shifting consumption to other time periods (load shifting);

  • Reducing or interrupting consumption (curtailment) or utilizing on-site generation in place of power from the grid (consumption of self-generated electricity).

Under a static perspective, consumers state or program their demand response flexibility as a set of constraints, and an energy management system aims to optimize the demand, storage and in-house generation with respect to these constraints. This is a stochastic dynamic optimization problem; the energy management system must repeatedly make decisions using estimations of the future states. If consumers have a volatile day-to-day schedule for their activities, one would expect that they either choose to devote time to systematically re-program their flexibility or choose their flexibility levels to adhere to the minimum ones that cover this volatility.

The MIRABEL EU project (Micro-Request-Based Aggregation, Forecasting and Scheduling of Energy Demand, Supply and Distribution) project was based on the concept of explicitly flexible demand and supply on the household level, encapsulated into flexibility objects (flex-objects). The concept of the flex-objects, as well as the algorithms developed by MIRABEL for aggregating, disaggregating and forecasting flex-objects, can be of high value when designing a demand response solution.

Under a more dynamic perspective, the interactions between occupant behavior, the weather, energy efficiency improvements and on-site generation capabilities should be tracked and understood, and the coordination between supply, demand and storage should be optimized accordingly.In this problem, data analytics do not aim to solve the optimization problem. One could use linear quadratic optimal control, $l_1$ regularization of the control law so that to create sparsity and, as a result, support decentralization, or a variant of reinforcement learning of the policy function. Data analytics stands between the real system and the controller and provides a function of summarizing the inputs into a reduced set of meaningful features and patterns with high predictive power.

In practical terms, data analytics can help simplify the function $g(\cdot)$ in the general formulation of optimal control, by simplifying the representation of states $x(t)$ and by helping define the way a control decision $u(t)$ affects the expected state.

x(t+1) = g(x(t), u(t))

s.t.

h(x(t), u(t))\leq0

A common approach in literature for supply, demand and storage coordination is based on the use of agent models. The rationale is that agent models provide for horizontal scaling, adaptability to consumer preferences or routines and privacy preservation. The downside is that the corresponding models rely on a market-based control scheme, which requires the simulation of a virtual day-ahead and intra-day market. The 'big data' technologies (see Spark or Hadoop) also provide for horizontal scalability. Ever more, a great number of machine learning algorithms are formulated as loss function minimization problems, something that involves repeatedly executing update equations. This brings adaptability to changing conditions. Concept Drift Adaptation approaches can enhance further the ability to flexibly react to such changes.

Analysing big data in a privacy-preserving fashion has been the focus of much on-going research. For example, the EU FP7 project AXLE (Advanced Analytics for Extremely Large European Databases) focuses on large-scale analytics on real-world datasets that include clinical data, while addressing the requirements of quality, privacy, security and auditability.

Automated demand response does not exclude providing consumers with feedback. On the contrary, if demand response aims at improving sufficiency and best use of available resources, consumers should be able to see how their actions contribute to the attainment of this goal. We are experimenting with approximations of the Shapley Value that learn it by associating the past actions of the consumers with the results at the aggregate level. The Shapley Value is the mean marginal contribution of each participant in a particular demand response scheme to all possible coalitions of the other participants.
