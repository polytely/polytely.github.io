Efficiency Oriented Energy Consumer Collaboration

Our basic principle is that intelligent energy efficiency is more about social innovations and technologies that enable their emergence and diffusion, and less about sensing and data analytics. One reason to start from this assumption is that social interactions and networks can create and evolve new ideas and new ways of doing things that have a higher rate of survival than ideas and products that are based on predefined perceptions of what people would or should do.

The second reason stems from the very nature of the transition towards a low-carbon energy system. Leaving aside the need for clean and flexible central supply, the utilization of decentralized and renewable-based generation is viable under a paradigm of local coordination of different capabilities (generation, efficient consumption and storage) with the aim of local energy sufficiency.

An energy community is a highly diverse set of consumers with varying capabilities; there are consumers that want to reduce energy consumption and costs, consumers with micro-generation capacity, even consumers with combined heat and power systems, which in combination with thermal storage, allow a time-flexible operation. This heterogeneity in capabilities is a strong driver for the community to evolve through interactions and experience sharing. It is interesting that this view of how an energy community can be defined mirrors the concept of microgrids. Microgrids achieve specific local goals, such as reliability, carbon emission reduction, diversification of energy sources, and cost reduction, established by the community being served.

We aim to develop the necessary infrastructure to perform scalable, adaptive learning of the consuming behaviour, efficiency improvements and generation capabilities of smart energy community members that collaborate to ensure quality of energy supply and environment preservation. The reason why we aim to explore the issue of consumer participation in the energy system transition by focusing on community formation is that while our behaviour in relation to energy consumption/prosumption and energy efficiency is affected by a series of interrelated factors, the creation and mobilization of communities around a shared goal, can affect a significant number of these factors.

In this context, 'learning' aims to understand and provide guidance, rather than forecasting. The notion of identifying inefficiencies by comparing the consumers’ profiles with the average profile of the cluster they belong to and the provision of recommendations based on changes that similar consumers were able to make, capitalize on the collaborative filtering concept in personalized recommendations systems. The basic idea of these systems can be described using the personalized recommendations generated by online bookstores: if users shared the same interests in the past – if they viewed or bought the same books, for instance – they will also have similar tastes in the future. The “collaborative filtering” name stems from the fact that the selection of hopefully interesting books involves filtering the most promising ones from a large set and that the users implicitly collaborate with one another.

In practice, clear-cut cluster structures are not the norm; there are consumers that could belong to more than one cluster. In this case, these consumers could be regarded as very efficient when compared to the average members of one cluster and very inefficient when compared to the average members of another. The recommendations functionality will capitalize on this reality, so that to always compare a consumer to the average one in the cluster where the consumer can be regarded as inefficient.
